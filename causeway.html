Causeway Case Study - Causeway is a responsive web application built for crowdfunding sustainable and eco-friendly causes.

Role

Research, Branding, UX/UI, Wireframe/Prototype

Causeway is a responsive, web app built for funding sustainable causes – Harnessing social media to help fund projects near to those organizations who create them.

The Challenge

Taming endless webs of information, organizing files and creating documents often takes time away from real work creatives and business owners need to accomplish.

Causeway aims to resolve these issues by providing an accessible, easy-to-use solution, with a simple UI that lets the user collect various files and info in a single place.

The
Process

With the problem defined, we take the next step in the design process, in the planning phase. The goal here is to understand the typical Blocbox user and determine their requirements.

Creating a survey using multiple-choice and short-answer questions we gathered info from potential users to learn user browsing behaviors, how they save and share content, as well as what apps they use to write or makes notes online. The results and responses are summarized to inform our design process and to find potential patterns.

User Research

In order to understand my users in the most thorough way possible, I developed and distributed a survey across social media channels aimed at individuals that find themselves in collaborative situations. I aimed to discover:

  1. Technology users employed to access the internet
  2. Behavior of users once they have found a valuable link or asset
  3. How users collaborate with others online in their personal and professional work
  4. Programs and apps users employ to take notes, save links and images

Analyzing the responses received from the surveys we sent out, all own a smart phone, tablet or laptop. The majority of users browse content online with mobile devices, while a little over one-third use a tablet or laptop.

Social Media websites were the most popular content browsed online, with news ranking second. Only a quarter of the applicants surveyed save links or images they find online. Over half save content occasionally. Managing bookmarks and easily grouping content easily will be a focus of our Blocbox dashboard moving forward.

Competitive
Analysis

To help determine how Causeway measures up against competition, we took a closer look at our requirements for the web app, comparing them to similar features in competitor’s applications, like Dropbox, Google Drive and Box.

This will help us determine how our product is different than those made by Dropbox and Box. More importantly, we can solve measurable usability issues by performing specific tasks, meeting user requirements that will help us stand apart from competitors.

User
Personas

Expanding on the data received from our surveys, follow-up interviews were done with several subjects to expand on answers given and provide insight into the behavior of our users. This also allowed us to determine if Blocbox is a natural fit into user workflows while keeping empathy intertwined in the design process.

User
Flows

Based on the Causeway business requirements and our user research, we can determine a list of user stories that will help us create user flows. The user flows help us identify potential pain points for users and avoid forgetting key screens or features later in the design process.

We focused on new and returning users. Each having different paths to take in order to achieve goals for specific tasks.

Logo /
Branding

The goal of the Blocbox branding was to create a simple, yet memorable brand identity, pointing to the ease-of-use of the product, and maintaining the same straightforward and clean philosophy that guided the user experience.

Wireframes

Using quick paper sketches as a guide for structure and layout, Balsamiq was then used to quickly build low-fidelity, interactive prototypes.

Illustrator and InVision were important tools used to create hi-fidelity wireframes of the landing page, on-boarding, dashboard and account settings, in order to receive feedback from users right away.

User Testing

Utilizing Invision, we created working prototypes that were submitted for user testing. Early in the visual design phase, five second preference tests were used for initial feedback on our home page design and our branding, providing valuable information to guide future iterations.

Using Steve Krug’s, Rocket Surgery Made Easy as a guide, we created a brief usability test script to interview 15 men and women, ages 18-39, focusing on 3 tasks:

  1. Signing up for Blocbox
  2. Uploading content to Blocbox
  3. Changing account settings

Testing
Results

Using the Blocbox home page as a starting point each participant was asked to sign up for Blocbox, then to upload a set of photos to share with a client.

Of the 15 participants sampled, over half successfully completed the first task to sign up for the service, while one-third of the group failed. The average time taken to complete the initial task was 23 seconds.

The results of the second task were nearly identical to the first. Taking an average of ten seconds, over half of the participants successfully uploaded images to their account, while one-third were not successful and failed to complete their task.

The participants had much more success making changes to their account settings, each completing the task in an average of 5 seconds.

Final Observations

By asking questions early in the design process, we were able to learn how users share content online and observe their browsing behaviors.

Through our research and usability testing, we were able to identify user needs while still meeting goals and business requirements.

Learning which features users value most and which performed best impacted future product iterations. Ultimately, research put into the initial discovery and strategy phase resulted in a successful MVP that delivered requirements and that users appreciate.
